Bearla — Custom Heritage Keepsakes

Redesigning the custom keepsake experience to bridge the gap between abstract children's artwork and high-trust physical craftsmanship.

Role Lead Product Designer
Scope UX Research, Intent Capture UI, Mobile-First Web
Timeline 6 Weeks
Core Metric Goal Reduce Checkout Hesitation & Production Intent Loss

1. Empathize & Research Synthesis

Bearla is a specialized digital commerce experience that transforms raw children's drawings—like a grandchild's first scribble—into handcrafted precious metal keepsakes. To uncover the deep psychological barriers and hesitations behind custom keepsake shopping, I conducted mixed-methods user research targeting generational buyers.

Qualitative Interviews

5 in-depth remote interviews (30–45 min each) with parents and grandparents aged 34–68 who had either purchased or strongly considered purchasing custom keepsake jewelry in the past two years.

Quantitative Screener Survey

Targeted screener survey (n=24) to quantify hesitation factors around photo upload flows, non-refundable custom deposits, and visual fidelity expectations.

Persona Profile: Meredith, 61 — Generational Gift-Giver

Smartphone-Exclusive Buyer

“I want to give my daughter a piece that captures my grandson's imagination—not something where a meaningful detail was mistaken for a smudge and polished away.”

Device Context:

Smartphone-Exclusive (No Desktop PC). Manages all digital tasks on mobile, requiring a frictionless mobile upload path.

Emotional State & Friction:

High sentimental investment paired with transaction anxiety. Fears that an outside artisan will misinterpret abstract drawing elements.

Three Core Research Insights

  • Insight 1: The Subjective Ambiguity Gap
    79% of survey respondents expressed hesitation that artisans or automated systems wouldn't understand what specific lines or doodles represent without explicit intent notes.
  • Insight 2: Smartphone-First Capture & Multi-Device Responsive Mandate
    Photos of physical drawings sit exclusively on users' smartphones; the UI must support direct mobile camera scanning and gallery pickers while seamlessly adapting layouts across Mobile, Tablet, and Desktop viewports.
  • Insight 3: Transaction Anxiety in Non-Refundable Purchases
    4 of 5 qualitative interview participants explicitly stated they refuse to proceed to checkout without an upfront visual proof and intent confirmation preview prior to payment.

2. Define & Marketplace Gaps

Problem Statement: Meredith needs a way to see and trust how her grandson's drawing will become a finished piece before she commits to paying, because the emotional and financial stakes of a custom, irreversible purchase make uncertainty a dealbreaker.

Identifying Marketplace Gaps

  • 1. The Visualization Gap: Standard custom jewelry sites ask for payments upfront before showing how a 2D line drawing converts into a 3D physical piece. Bearla solves this by introducing a clear “Sketch-to-Gold” preview engine early in the flow.
  • 2. Mobile Upload Friction: Most users browse on mobile while photos of physical drawings sit on their phones. Standard sites force clunky multi-step desktop forms. Bearla streamlines the file-picker and camera upload UX directly within the primary shop flow.

How Might We Statements

  • How might we let someone preview the transformation before checkout, not after?
  • How might we make an imperfect phone photo of a drawing feel “good enough” to submit?
  • How might we help someone with zero jewelry-buying experience choose material and size confidently?

3. Ideate & User Personalization Architecture

Paper sketches and Crazy-8s explored three directions for the core upload-to-preview flow:

  • Direction A — Wizard-style form: Step-by-step questions before any visual feedback. Rejected — delays the “wow” moment too long, doesn't address trust gap.
  • Direction B — Instant AI-style preview: Show a rough live preview immediately after upload, before material/size selection. Chosen — front-loads reassurance.
  • Direction C — Gallery-first browsing: Show finished examples before asking for an upload. Kept as a supporting element (social proof), not the primary flow.

User Personalization Architecture

We mapped a 3-step continuous task flow designed to reduce drop-off during customization:

  • Step 1: Capture & Upload — Dual input options (Camera scan or gallery upload) with auto-contrast detection for hand-drawn lines.
  • Step 2: Material & Accent Selection — Real-time rendering selection across solid gold, silver, or resin color fills.
  • Step 3: Heirloom Proofing — Interactive 3D/high-res preview confirmation before cart entry.

4. Prototype & Responsive Grid Frameworks

Lo-fi wireframes: Grayscale wireframes tested the upload → preview → material selection sequence for basic navigability before visual design was applied.

Hi-fi prototype: Built out the full responsive flow in Figma, including the dual camera/gallery upload, live material swap, and heirloom proofing screen.

High-Fidelity Responsive Grid Frameworks

To preserve an editorial, high-end visual tone while adapting to mobile screens, we translated the desktop spatial system into touch-first mobile patterns:

  • Hero Transformation: Desktop split-screen (headline + side-by-side transformation preview) converts to a vertically stacked card module on mobile.
  • Navigation & CTAs: Desktop horizontal header links move into an accessible drawer; primary action buttons ('Upload Drawing', 'Begin Heirloom') lock to full-width touch targets (≥ 48px) on mobile viewports.

5. Systems Iteration & Defensive Controls

  • Phase 0 — MVP: Single upload button, no distinction between camera and gallery; users could only see the material preview after entering shipping details.
  • Phase 1 — The Upload Fix: Testing showed users didn't understand whether to photograph the drawing right then or find an existing photo. Added dual, clearly labeled entry points (“Take a Photo” / “Choose from Gallery”).
  • Phase 2 — The Preview Move: Moved the live material/color preview earlier in the flow, ahead of any shipping or payment fields, directly addressing the “trust precedes conversion” insight.
  • Phase 3 — The Crop Tool: Real drawings weren't single clean doodles — they were full pages with multiple scribbles. Added an inline crop/isolation tool so users could select just the part they wanted turned into jewelry.

Defensive System Controls & Edge-Case Logic

Handling user-generated artwork introduces critical edge cases. We designed specific system prompts and validation flows for:

  • Low-Resolution / Blurry Uploads: Auto-detecting faint pencil lines and prompting users to adjust lighting or trace over the sketch.
  • Cropping & Line Isolation: Providing an inline vector-crop tool so users can isolate a single doodle from a full page of scribbles.

6. Validation & Usability Testing

Methodology: Moderated usability testing with 5 participants (ages 42–67, none jewelry-industry professionals) using the hi-fi prototype. Task: “Upload a photo of a child's drawing, select a material, and reach the order-confirmation preview screen.”

  • P0 — Insight 1: The Upload Ambiguity
    The Data: 4 of 5 participants hesitated for 8+ seconds at the original single-button upload screen, unsure if they should photograph the page live or search their camera roll.
    The Impact: Validated the Phase 1 dual-entry-point fix; on re-test, all 5 participants selected an option within 2 seconds.
  • P1 — Insight 2: Cluttered Drawings
    The Data: 3 of 5 participants uploaded a photo containing more than one drawing on the page and were unsure how the system would interpret it.
    The Impact: Directly justified the Phase 3 crop tool — without it, ambiguous uploads risked producing a keepsake of the wrong doodle entirely.
  • P2 — Insight 3: Trust Signals Before Payment
    The Data: All 5 participants said seeing the material preview before being asked for shipping/payment info made them “much more comfortable” continuing.
    The Impact: Confirmed the Phase 2 reordering and shaped the broader “Trust Precedes Conversion” takeaway.

7. Inclusive Layout Hierarchy & Strategic Takeaways

Inclusive Layout & Accessibility

  • Contrast & Legibility: Maintained strict WCAG AA contrast on warm neutral backgrounds, ensuring legibility for older demographics (e.g., grandparents purchasing gifts).
  • Screen-Reader Hierarchy: Structured semantic heading levels (H1, H2, H3) and alt-text descriptions for interactive transformation previews.

Strategic Product Takeaways

  • Trust Precedes Conversion: For highly custom, emotional products, showing the transformation process before forcing account creation or checkout is essential for driving conversions.
  • Responsive Cohesion: Designing modular grid components made it seamless to scale rich brand storytelling from desktop down to mobile viewports without sacrificing performance or clarity.
