Bearla — Custom Heritage Keepsakes

Turning Raw Children's Doodles into Solid Gold Heirloom Brooches

Project: Responsive E-Commerce Platform
Role: Lead Product Designer
Timeline: 6 Weeks

1. Empathize

Research Approach: To understand hesitation around custom jewelry purchases, I ran 5 remote interviews (30 min each) with parents and grandparents aged 34–68 who had either purchased or considered purchasing a custom keepsake piece in the past two years, plus a short screener survey (n=22) to validate recurring themes.

Key Persona: Meredith, 61 — First-Time Custom Gift Buyer

“I want to give my daughter something she'll cry happy tears over — not something that looks like a random squiggle stamped onto a pendant.”

Meredith wants to turn her grandson's drawing into a necklace for her daughter's birthday. She's bought jewelry online before, but never anything custom. She's price-sensitive about a purchase she can't undo, and she doesn't trust that a rough crayon scribble can become something that actually looks intentional.

Recurring Pain Points from Research

  • Fear of an expensive mistake: 18 of 22 survey respondents said “not knowing what I'd actually get” was their top hesitation with custom jewelry sites.
  • Upload anxiety: Most drawings exist only as phone photos with shadows, torn edges, or multiple doodles per page; participants weren't sure what counted as “usable.”
  • No sense of scale or material: People couldn't picture whether a design would work better in gold, silver, or resin, or at what size.

2. Define

Problem Statement: Meredith needs a way to see and trust how her grandson's drawing will become a finished piece before she commits to paying, because the emotional and financial stakes of a custom, irreversible purchase make uncertainty a dealbreaker.

How Might We Statements

  • How might we let someone preview the transformation before checkout, not after?
  • How might we make an imperfect phone photo of a drawing feel “good enough” to submit?
  • How might we help someone with zero jewelry-buying experience choose material and size confidently?

3. Ideate

Paper sketches and Crazy-8s explored three directions for the core upload-to-preview flow:

  • Direction A — Wizard-style form: Step-by-step questions before any visual feedback. Rejected — delays the “wow” moment too long, doesn't address trust gap.
  • Direction B — Instant AI-style preview: Show a rough live preview immediately after upload, before material/size selection. Chosen — front-loads reassurance.
  • Direction C — Gallery-first browsing: Show finished examples before asking for an upload. Kept as a supporting element (social proof), not the primary flow.

Converging on Direction B led to the “Sketch-to-Gold” preview engine as the spine of the flow — refined below.

5. Systems Iteration — From MVP to Refined Flow

  • Phase 0 — MVP: Single upload button, no distinction between camera and gallery; users could only see the material preview after entering shipping details.
  • Phase 1 — The Upload Fix: Testing showed users didn't understand whether to photograph the drawing right then or find an existing photo. Added dual, clearly labeled entry points (“Take a Photo” / “Choose from Gallery”).
  • Phase 2 — The Preview Move: Moved the live material/color preview earlier in the flow, ahead of any shipping or payment fields, directly addressing the “trust precedes conversion” insight.
  • Phase 3 — The Crop Tool: Real drawings weren't single clean doodles — they were full pages with multiple scribbles. Added an inline crop/isolation tool so users could select just the part they wanted turned into jewelry.

6. Validation & Usability Testing

Methodology: Moderated usability testing with 5 participants (ages 42–67, none jewelry-industry professionals) using the hi-fi prototype. Task: “Upload a photo of a child's drawing, select a material, and reach the order-confirmation preview screen.”

  • P0 — Insight 1: The Upload Ambiguity
    The Data: 4 of 5 participants hesitated for 8+ seconds at the original single-button upload screen, unsure if they should photograph the page live or search their camera roll.
    The Impact: Validated the Phase 1 dual-entry-point fix; on re-test, all 5 participants selected an option within 2 seconds.
  • P1 — Insight 2: Cluttered Drawings
    The Data: 3 of 5 participants uploaded a photo containing more than one drawing on the page and were unsure how the system would interpret it.
    The Impact: Directly justified the Phase 3 crop tool — without it, ambiguous uploads risked producing a keepsake of the wrong doodle entirely.
  • P2 — Insight 3: Trust Signals Before Payment
    The Data: All 5 participants said seeing the material preview before being asked for shipping/payment info made them “much more comfortable” continuing.
    The Impact: Confirmed the Phase 2 reordering and shaped the broader “Trust Precedes Conversion” takeaway.

7. Accessibility & Strategic Takeaways

Accessibility Compliance

  • Contrast & Legibility: Maintained strict WCAG AA contrast on warm neutral backgrounds, ensuring legibility for older demographics.
  • Screen-Reader Hierarchy: Structured semantic heading levels (H1, H2, H3) and alt-text descriptions for interactive previews.

Product Design Takeaways

  • Trust Precedes Conversion: Showing the transformation process before forcing account creation or checkout is essential for driving conversions.
  • Responsive Cohesion: Designing modular grid components made it seamless to scale rich brand storytelling down to mobile.
